What influenced and inspired you to learn to code?

My first experience with code was in middle school. I had a technology class
where we dabbled in several different softwares: Dreamweaver, Microworlds,
Flash. Not all of these were ‘real’ programming, mind you, but they were a
taste of what programming could do. From HTML and CSS in Dreamweaver, using
actionscript in Flash, or giving commands to make a little turtle move around
and draw things in Microworlds. I wanted to continue learning and even looked
at high schools that had programming classes. One high school, bronx science,
quickly becamse one of my top choices for that reason, and I tried downloading
a Java IDE and to try to learn Java myself in the meantime. I didn’t get far at
that point, not without guidance. I ended up going to a different high school,
which had great courses in engineering, but not so much in computer science. So
that was the end of my venture into programming in gradeschool.

Preoccupied with other things, I did not resume trying to learn programming
until I in college. Starting undergrad at Carnegie Mellon, I became friends
with many computer science majors, and having a natural curiousity for the
subject, I decided in fall of my sophomore year to take a computer science
class. It was a Python course, called Fundamentals of Programming. The school
had, that semester, split the course into two different versions: one which
was oriented towards non-majors, who perhaps had to take the class as a
requirement, and CS majors, who would be given a more intensive and in depth
experience, since they needed to have a solid foundation for CS before moving
further with their degree. The former was taught in Ruby, the latter in
Python, and I decided that I would take the harder one, if for no other reason
then I wanted the challenge, and also, that I had never heard of Ruby (the
irony is that I now know alot more Ruby than I do Python because of Rails). So
I began taking the class, and it ended up being one of the best courses I ever
took. The professor was so engaging and such a fantastic lecturer that I liken
him to Robin William’s character in The Dead Poets Society. You would think
that a CS lecture would be kind of dry, at best going over some key concepts
on a chalkboard, but these lectures, with this professor, had amazing programs
and come alive before your very eyes, he even started writing a whole new
programming language, in python, during a lecture. It was like watching one of
those cooking shows, where you really get to see someone make a recipe in a
way that lets you follow it later. It is because of this class that I really
fell in love with programming.

After that class I took the next programming course in the sequence, but
due to my involvement in extracurricular activities, lackluster teaching (the
polar opposite of the professor in the first course, and the dullest lectures
I’ve had the displeasure to sit through), also it was in C programming
language (or a subset created for the class called c0), I did not do well.
While after the first class I started thinking about a CS minor, after the
second I was discouraged from pursuing programming any further.

But something about my interest in programming just won’t quit. A year later
I once again found myself teaching myself some programming. This time in my
senior year. I decided to teach myself some C. I practiced by writing programs
to answer math problems from projecteuler.net. This was in the middle of a
semester and proved to be a rather slow way to progress in programming. C is a
whole different animal from Ruby and Python, and while it’s a great way to
learn how the computer itself works, its difficult to actually build anything
which means its rather hard to teach to yourself. I hope to go back to C some
day and really learn it, but for now I’m staying in the domain of higher level
languages.

The following semester I started learning Ruby. My previous stint learning
Ruby had been that same semester that I took that awful course in C0. I had
first tried to learn Ruby by joining the webteams of of the student theatre
organization on campus. The website was in Ruby on Rails. And for some reason
I was too impatient to take my time with Ruby first, and decided to dive right
into Rails. Needless to say, I was lost. Despite the similarities between Ruby
and Python, with which I was already familiar, I didn’t know enough Ruby to
actually understand what was happening in a Rails app. I didnt really grasp
the concept of MVC either. There were enough people on the webteam who already
knew Ruby and Rails that they didn’t really spend much time teach the few of
us who were new (I hear this changed later on, when the webteam got better
about training people under new leadership). But at the time I just kind of
tried to follow along, but ultimately everything was over my head and I
stopped going.

This time I was doing things differently. I would learn Ruby first, grasp
the fundamentals, get comfortable with the syntax and the heavier
object-orientedness compared with Python (python is pretty much equally
capable of being object oriented, but my course in python did not delve too
deeply into that). I used websites like rubymonk.com and codeacademy.com
to get a grasp of what ruby really did. I also learned some JQuery and messed
with common website features in javascript (sticky menu bars,
animated buttons, dropdown menus), from scratch. After learning ruby for about
a month I jumped into creating a Rails app. It was an app to replace the
shift management system for one of my campus jobs as a residence hall desk
attendant, and I really started to get the hang of what it involves to create
a web application (and how tedious it is to work with HTML and CSS, even with
bootstrap). After that things started moving more quickly. I had graduated,
moved back to NYC, started going to programming related meetups, job fairs,
found an internship working on an online marketplace for video game fan art. I
ultimately decided this is what I want to do, but that I could use some more
in depth experience, for which I turned to Fullstack Academy. After some
coding interviews where I am pretty sure I started out seeming slow, I was
accepted and that led me to write this. I can’t wait to learn a Node based
application stack, something about it just seems to have what Rails lacks.
Rails is fine, but it does alot for you, and going to a Rails bootcamp, where
there was no guarantee of delving deeper into the mechanics of building a good
web app didn’t seem like the best use of my time and money (and since I
already knew some rails, and bootcamps are usually geared towards complete
beginners), I figured I would apply to a bootcamp that would teach me
something both knew, and cutting edge. I can’t wait to get started.
